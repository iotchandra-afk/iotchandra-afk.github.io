DATA & EVIDENCE

The Data Problem Hiding Under Every AI Strategy

Enterprises often have data. What they lack is the context, ownership, evidence, and workflow access required for responsible action.

Chandra KanojiaPublished July 5, 2026 · 8 min read

Many AI strategies begin with a familiar diagnosis: the organization needs better data. That is true, but incomplete. The problem is rarely only volume, storage, or model access. The harder gap is operational context.

AI needs to know what the data means in the work, who owns it, when it is trustworthy, what decision it may support, and what evidence must accompany the action.

Data without context creates ambiguity

A customer, claim, account, case, interaction, or employee can be represented differently across systems. Each representation may be technically valid while still being insufficient for a decision. The enterprise must resolve identity, timing, provenance, definitions, and business meaning.

Without that context, AI can produce fluent outputs that are difficult to trust or act on.

Ownership matters more than cataloging

Catalogs and lineage tools are useful, but action requires ownership. Someone must be accountable for the definition, quality, permitted use, and change path of the data that enters the workflow.

Ownership becomes especially important when AI combines sources that were created for different purposes or governance regimes.

Evidence must travel with the decision

In regulated work, an answer is not enough. The organization may need to know which sources were used, whether they were current, which policy applied, what exceptions existed, and whether a person reviewed the action.

Evidence should be designed into the flow so that it can support execution, audit, monitoring, and learning.

Data must reach the point of work

Enterprise data programs can succeed technically while failing operationally. A well-governed data product that is difficult to access inside the workflow will not change behavior. Teams will continue to search, copy, reconcile, and create local workarounds.

The value of data depends on its availability in the moment of decision.

A better data question

Instead of asking whether the organization is “AI ready,” ask whether a specific workflow has the trusted context required for a specific decision. That turns an abstract data transformation into a bounded operating problem.

  • What context does the decision require?
  • Who owns the meaning and quality of that context?
  • What evidence must be retained?
  • Where does the data enter the workflow?
  • How will changes be monitored and governed?

AI strategy becomes executable when data is treated not only as an asset, but as context and evidence inside the operating model.

When this becomes relevant

Use this argument when the organization is producing transformation activity but cannot yet show that the operating constraint, decision model, or economics of work have changed.

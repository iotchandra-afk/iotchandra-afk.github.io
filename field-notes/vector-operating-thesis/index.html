OPERATING MODEL

Aligning Capabilities Around the Constraint: The VECTOR Operating Thesis

Transformation creates value when the enterprise aligns its capabilities around the constraint that matters most, then carries that alignment through evidence, adoption, and scale.

Chandra KanojiaPublished July 5, 2026 · 9 min read

Most enterprises do not suffer from a lack of capability. They have strategies, platforms, data, talented people, governance forums, transformation portfolios, and more pilots than they can comfortably count. Value still remains trapped because those capabilities are rarely aligned around the same constraint.

That is the central idea behind VECTOR. The framework is not a new layer of methodology. It is a way to hold six necessary disciplines together so that strategy, technology, operations, governance, and change do not travel on parallel tracks.

V: Start with the value-creation imperative

The first question is not, “Where can we apply AI?” It is, “What is preventing the business from creating the next unit of value?” The constraint may be advisor capacity, slow claims decisions, fragmented service work, weak data context, a control bottleneck, or a platform that records activity without guiding action.

A value-creation imperative creates discipline. It names the outcome, identifies the binding constraint, and prevents the organization from mistaking technology activity for business progress.

E: Evolve the operating model

Technology changes little when the work around it remains fixed. Roles, decision rights, controls, incentives, escalation paths, and customer journeys must change with the capability. Otherwise, the enterprise adds a new interface to an old operating model.

This is why task automation often disappoints. A task may become faster while the end-to-end flow remains burdened by handoffs, queues, exceptions, and unclear ownership. The local gain is real, but the enterprise constraint does not move.

C: Coordinate enterprise capabilities

Material outcomes sit across organizational boundaries. Product defines the experience. Engineering builds the capability. Data provides context. Operations carries the work. Risk and governance define the boundaries. Change enables people to work differently. Value appears only when these groups are aligned around the same outcome and sequence of decisions.

Coordination is not more governance. It is fewer competing definitions of success.

T: Translate strategy into execution

Executive intent must become a sequence of choices that teams can act on. What happens first? What must be true before the next release? Which decisions are reversible? Where is human judgment mandatory? What must change in the operating routine before the technology can scale?

Translation is the bridge between a compelling strategic narrative and accountable execution in live operations.

O: Optimize through evidence

Evidence is the discipline that keeps transformation honest. It should answer whether the constraint moved, whether behavior changed, whether the customer or employee experience improved, whether controls held, and whether the intervention deserves more investment.

The most useful evidence is not a dashboard of delivery activity. It is a small set of operating measures tied directly to the value-creation imperative.

R: Realize and scale value

Launch is not realization. The gains must survive changes in volume, staffing, leadership attention, market conditions, and customer behavior. That requires reliability, governance, ownership, operating routines, and adoption that persist beyond the project team.

Scale should follow evidence. The enterprise expands what works, changes what does not, and stops work that cannot move the constraint.

The practical test

VECTOR is useful when a transformation feels busy but economically unclear. Ask six questions: Is the value imperative explicit? Has the operating model changed? Are the necessary capabilities coordinated? Has strategy been translated into accountable execution? Is evidence testing the real constraint? Are the gains designed to survive and scale?

When any one of these is missing, the organization may still deliver technology. It is less likely to deliver durable operating value.

When this becomes relevant

Use this argument when the organization is producing transformation activity but cannot yet show that the operating constraint, decision model, or economics of work have changed.

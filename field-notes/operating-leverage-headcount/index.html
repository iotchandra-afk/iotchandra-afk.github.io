OPERATING LEVERAGE

When Growth Is Still Tied to Headcount, AI Has Not Changed the Operating Model

Task productivity is not operating leverage. The real test is whether the binding constraint moved and whether the economics of work changed.

Chandra KanojiaPublished July 5, 2026 · 8 min read

Many AI programs can demonstrate that an individual task is faster. Fewer can show that the business can grow, serve, decide, or manage risk without adding proportional complexity and headcount.

That distinction matters. Productivity improves a task. Operating leverage changes the relationship between growth and the resources required to support it.

Why task gains disappear

A task-level gain often lands inside an unchanged flow of work. The employee completes one step faster, then waits for missing context, hands the case to another team, resolves an exception outside the system, or seeks approval through the same management layers as before.

The saved minutes are real, but they do not automatically become capacity. The enterprise may absorb them as more volume, more coordination, or more work in progress.

Find the binding constraint

Every operating system has a constraint that limits throughput or value. In a service environment, it may be expert availability. In a claims process, it may be evidence collection. In a wealth business, it may be advisor capacity. In an enterprise platform, it may be data quality, control review, or adoption.

The objective is not to remove every constraint. Constraints persist. The objective is to shift the binding constraint so the business has more room to operate and grow.

Redesign the flow, not only the task

Operating leverage usually requires several changes at once. Work must be simplified and standardized. Data and decision support must arrive inside the workflow. Roles and escalation paths must be redesigned. Exceptions must be visible. Controls must be proportionate to risk. Performance measures must reward the end-to-end outcome, not only local activity.

AI becomes useful when it supports that redesign. It can prepare context, classify work, recommend next actions, draft communications, detect risk, or automate low-risk decisions. But the operating model determines whether those capabilities reduce friction or simply add another layer.

Measure the economics of work

The most important measures are not the number of models, pilots, or users. They are the measures that show whether the economics changed: cost to serve, throughput, cycle time, rework, exception rates, expert capacity, customer effort, control quality, and the amount of growth that can be absorbed without proportional staffing.

The measure should follow the constraint. A broad efficiency target can hide the fact that the true bottleneck remains untouched.

What leaders should ask

  • Which constraint limits the next unit of growth or value?
  • Where does work wait, repeat, or leave the system?
  • Which decisions require expert judgment, and which do not?
  • What operating behavior must change for the technology to matter?
  • How will we know that capacity was actually created?

AI has changed the operating model when the business can do more than complete a task faster. It can absorb more demand, improve quality, preserve control, and create growth without recreating the same constraint elsewhere.

When this becomes relevant

Use this argument when the organization is producing transformation activity but cannot yet show that the operating constraint, decision model, or economics of work have changed.
